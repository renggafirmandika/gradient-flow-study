COMP5329 / COMP4329 Deep Learning Assignment 2: Course Conference Project USYD Deep Learning Course Conference 2026 (OpenReview) 1. Overview In this assignment, students will complete a research-oriented project related to topics covered in this course and present their work in the form of an academic paper. All projects must be completed in groups of 2–3 students. Each group is expected to formulate a clear research question and carry out a complete research workflow, including problem formulation, method design, experimental analysis, and academic writing. This assignment emphasizes research thinking, technical rigor, and scholarly communication, rather than purely engineering implementation or performance optimization. The final paper will be submitted via OpenReview and evaluated using a double-blind peer review process. 2. Project Scope and Expectations This assignment is designed as a research-oriented project that synthesizes the theoretical foundations and practical techniques covered throughout the course. Unlike structured implementation tasks, this project requires students to formulate their own research questions, design appropriate methodologies, and communicate their findings in a formal academic format. The following sections outline the scope of acceptable projects, clarify expectations regarding hypothesis-driven research, and describe the support mechanisms available during project development. Together, these guidelines define the intellectual standards and methodological rigor expected of all submissions. 2.1 Project Focus and Example Topics The primary objective of this assignment is to engage students in a research-oriented
investigation centered on deep learning. Projects should examine deep learning methods, model architectures, training mechanisms, or learning behaviors at a conceptual or algorithmic level. Both theory-driven and application-motivated projects are acceptable; however, the central contribution must advance understanding of deep learning itself rather than merely demonstrate its use. Projects that consist solely of literature review, straightforward re-implementation of existing methods, or simple performance comparisons without deeper methodological or mechanistic analysis will not be considered sufficiently research-oriented. While empirical evaluation is essential, numerical improvement alone does not constitute a meaningful research contribution. To clarify this distinction, consider two contrasting examples. A project that applies standard neural architectures to a molecular toxicity classification task and reports that one model outperforms another primarily demonstrates application-level performance differences. Although such work may be technically correct, it does not substantially investigate model behavior or learning mechanisms. In contrast, a project that designs an adaptive Transformer architecture whose computational depth varies according to molecular structure, and systematically analyzes trade-offs between computation cost, memory usage, and predictive accuracy, engages directly with architectural design and computational principles. Even though the project is motivated by an application, its primary contribution lies in understanding and improving deep learning mechanisms. Students are free to choose their own topics. The course may provide suggested ideas for inspiration, but these do not constrain project selection. Regardless of topic, a strong project must go beyond reproduction and aim to generate new analytical insights, methodological refinements, or systematic empirical understanding. 2.2 Hypothesis-Driven Research Research is inherently uncertain, particularly within the limited duration of a semester. Meaningful research questions often involve ambiguity, risk, and the possibility of inconclusive or negative outcomes. If the results of a project can be predicted in advance, the underlying question is likely insufficiently ambitious or lacks depth. Students are therefore encouraged to formulate clear and focused hypotheses that can be examined empirically or analytically. A well-scoped project typically centers on a single core research question rather than attempting to address multiple loosely connected ideas. Projects should articulate the motivation behind the hypothesis, specify how it will be tested, and define appropriate evaluation criteria. Negative results, when rigorously analyzed and properly interpreted, are considered
valid and valuable contributions. Conversely, strong empirical improvements are not persuasive unless accompanied by careful discussion of their statistical reliability, practical implications, limitations, and scope of applicability. The emphasis throughout this assignment is on intellectual rigor, clarity of reasoning, and depth of analysis rather than superficial performance gains. 2.3 Project Discussion and Guidance Students are encouraged to consult with course tutors during the early stages of project formulation. Discussions may focus on clarifying the research question, refining the scope of the project, assessing feasibility within the semester timeline, or evaluating the methodological soundness of the proposed approach. Project groups are not restricted to a specific tutor; consultation may take place in any tutorial session attended by at least one group member. All tutors involved in the course are PhD graduates or current PhD candidates specializing in deep learning or closely related fields, and are equipped to provide research-oriented guidance. Early discussion is strongly recommended, particularly for projects involving novel architectural modifications, theoretical analysis, or substantial experimental design, where feasibility and clarity of scope are critical to success. 3.Submission Requirements 3.1 Submission Format and Templates The final deliverable for this assignment is a research paper written in the style of a machine learning conference publication. The paper should present the project’s motivation, methodology, experimental findings, and analytical insights in a structured and academically rigorous manner. Informal reports, technical logs, or presentation-style documents will not be considered acceptable substitutes. All submissions must be made through the designated OpenReview venue. The final version must be uploaded in PDF format and comply with the formatting requirements specified by the course. The course provides an official LaTeX template and an official Word template. Students are required to prepare their manuscript using one of these templates. Adherence to the provided formatting guidelines is considered part of the overall assessment of professionalism and compliance.
The LaTeX template is strongly recommended. Students are encouraged to use Overleaf or another collaborative LaTeX platform to facilitate structured writing, version control, and collaborative editing. Proper use of the template will help ensure consistent formatting of figures, tables, equations, and references. 3.2 Recommended Paper Structure The manuscript should follow the conventions of academic research papers in machine learning. While minor structural adaptations are permitted where justified, the overall organization is expected to resemble a standard conference paper. A typical structure includes an abstract summarizing the core contribution, an introduction motivating the research question, a related work section positioning the project within existing literature, a methods or approach section detailing the proposed methodology, an experiments and results section presenting empirical findings, a conclusion summarizing key insights, and a complete list of references. The clarity of organization and logical coherence across sections are important evaluation criteria. The paper should present a clear research narrative rather than a collection of disconnected technical components. 3.3 Length Requirements The main body of the paper must not exceed eight pages in the specified double-column conference format. A length between six and eight pages is recommended, as this range typically allows sufficient depth without unnecessary verbosity. Appendices are permitted and do not count toward the page limit. They may be used to provide additional experimental details, supplementary analyses, extended result tables, or mathematical derivations that support the main text. However, all core contributions, primary experimental findings, and central arguments must appear within the main body of the paper. Reviewers are not guaranteed to examine appendices in detail. Conciseness, clarity, and prioritization of essential content are part of the expected scholarly standard. 3.4 OpenReview Submission Guidelines All group members must register for an OpenReview account prior to submission. Author information must be entered accurately at the time of abstract submission. Students should carefully verify author order and spelling, as modifications to the author list are not permitted after the abstract deadline.
 Students are responsible for ensuring that the correct PDF file is uploaded and that all metadata is properly completed in the submission system. Failure to comply with OpenReview requirements may result in administrative complications or penalties. 4.Late Submission Assignment 2 will be released on Friday, 6 March. Use of AI Assistants Contemporary AI systems have reached a level of maturity that makes them widely adopted in computer science research and engineering practice. Leading academic researchers and professional software engineers routinely employ AI tools to assist with code development, experimental design, literature exploration, and academic writing. The integration of such tools into technical workflows is an irreversible trend. In alignment with this reality, the course does not impose restrictions on the use of AI tools for this assignment. Students are permitted to use AI systems at any stage of their work, including but not limited to code implementation, research ideation, experimental
planning, and report writing. Learning how to effectively and responsibly leverage AI assistance is itself considered an important professional skill. However, the use of AI tools does not transfer responsibility for correctness, rigor, or originality. AI-generated content may contain hallucinations, superficial reasoning, incorrect technical statements, fabricated experimental interpretations, or non-existent references. Overly generic analysis or stylistic fluency without substantive insight will not be rewarded. If inaccuracies, unsupported claims, or fabricated citations appear in a submission, marks will be deducted accordingly. Students remain fully accountable for the validity, precision, and depth of their work, regardless of whether AI tools were used in its preparation. The evaluation criteria defined in the grading rubric apply without exception. 6.Grading Rubric Evaluation of Assignment 2 is based primarily on the scholarly quality of the submitted paper. The assessment criteria reflect standards commonly applied in machine learning conferences, with emphasis on intellectual contribution, methodological rigor, and clarity of presentation. Grading will focus on the main body of the paper. While tutors will review appendices to better understand technical details and supplementary analyses, peer reviewers are not guaranteed to examine supplementary materials in depth. Accordingly, all central ideas, methodological innovations, experimental findings, and core conclusions must be presented clearly within the main paper. The appendix should serve a supporting role rather than containing essential arguments. The grading rubric is structured as follows. A. Paper Evaluation (90 pts) Criterion High-Score Guidelines Novelty & Significance (25) The project demonstrates clear research novelty or provides new scientific insights. It contributes meaningfully to understanding deep learning methods, mechanisms, or behaviors. The work goes beyond simple re-implementation or superficial performance comparison. For application-driven projects, the application must yield insight into deep learning itself rather than merely demonstrating empirical improvement. Soundness & Content (30) Methods and analyses are technically sound and correctly implemented. Conclusions are supported by appropriate experimental or analytical evidence. Experiments are well
designed, include fair comparisons and suitable baselines, and where appropriate, contain ablation studies, hyperparameter analysis, or uncertainty discussion. The scope of the project is coherent and proportionate to the course timeframe. Clarity & Presentation (20) The paper is logically structured and clearly written. A well-defined research question or central hypothesis is articulated. Motivation is coherent and analysis is thoughtful. Figures and tables are informative and properly integrated into the argument. Results are discussed honestly, including limitations and implications. Review & Positioning (20) Relevant prior work is appropriately reviewed and accurately cited. The project is clearly positioned relative to existing literature. The paper explains how the work extends, refines, challenges, or contextualizes prior research, and acknowledges assumptions and limitations. The main paper adheres to the page limit. B. Bonus (up to 10 points) In addition to the evaluation, bonus marks may be awarded through two mechanisms. An optional end-of-semester oral presentation may earn up to 10 additional points at the discretion of the lecturer, based on clarity, depth, and professionalism of delivery. Furthermore, each registered student will be assigned two peer reviews through OpenReview. High-quality reviews—defined by specificity, constructiveness, and technical relevance—will be eligible for bonus marks. Each high-quality review may earn 5 points, with a maximum of two reviews counted per student. Review quality will be assessed by the teaching team. Superficial or generic comments will not qualify for bonus credit.
